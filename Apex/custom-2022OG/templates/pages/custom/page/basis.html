A Revolutionary Way to Tackle Heel Pain

There’s nothing worse than being unable to relax at home.

After a long day of work as a middle school librarian, Phyllis always looked forward to coming home, kicking off her shoes and relaxing.

Except she couldn't relax – her feet were in too much pain.

Like many people her age, Phyllis suffered from plantar fasciitis, one of the most common causes of heel and foot pain. And while she seemed to do well during the day when she could wear a pair of supportive shoes with orthotic inserts, things seemed to fall apart each evening when she took her shoes off and walked around the house barefooted.

When Going Barefoot is a Pain

Plantar fasciitis or heel pain is caused when the arch doesn’t receive enough support and collapses. When this happens, the bundle of tendons, ligaments and muscles that make up the arch are stretched to an unnatural position, causing pain at the heel.

Typically, improper footwear or worn out footwear are the most likely culprits.

The cruel irony of it all is that even though plantar fasciitis or heel pain can be caused by the wrong pair of shoes, skipping shoes altogether and going barefoot in the evening can make matters worse. That’s because you’re switching from wearing shoes that provide poor support for the arches to wearing nothing, which provides zero support.

In fact, the experts at Cedars-Sinai and the University of Michigan both say that going barefoot can make the pain worse. The University of Michigan even goes so far as to recommend putting on “your shoes as soon as you get out of bed” in the morning to avoid the pain.

Which is a shame for people, like Phyllis, who are dealing with plantar fasciitis, because it’s hard to feel like you’re relaxing at home when you have to choose between keeping your shoes on (and presumably off the furniture) or having feet that hurt. Putting on a pair of shoes first thing after you wake up? There has to be a better solution.

What About Slippers?

That’s good question, and it’s what Phyllis wondered one evening as she was vacuuming up grass she had accidently tracked into her bedroom with her shoes.

After all, wearing a slipper must be better than wearing nothing, right?

Not really. Slippers generally aren’t made to provide much arch support, as most of them are made with cloth or thin rubber soles. They’re generally made to keep your feet warm and look cute, but from an arch support standpoint, most slippers are no different than going barefoot.

"Going barefoot or wearing slippers may make your pain worse." – University of Michigan Health System

After purchasing a stylish pair of slippers from her local Big Box Retailer, Phyllis was eager to try them out when she got home. Much to her disappointment, the pain persisted, and she was right back where she started.

“What if there was a slipper that had the footbed and arch support of a shoe?” she wondered to herself.
