How Shoes Can Help Fight a National Health Crisis

Falls among the elderly are more serious than you think.

The first time Janice’s father fell, she knew they got lucky: no broken bones or injuries, just some bruises and a sore knee. But Janice also knew that when – not if – the next phone call came, her father may not be so lucky. A broken hip? Having to move into an assisted living facility? Something much worse?

 
"Falls result in more than 2.8 million injuries treated in emergency departments each year, including over 800,000 hospitalizations and more than 27,000 deaths." – National Council on Aging
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
 

Unfortunately, these are the sorts of questions that many families face each day. According to the National Council on Aging, an older adult is treated for a fall in the emergency room every 11 seconds.

A National Health Crisis…

What Janice and her father are dealing with are part of a much larger national health crisis of falls among the elderly population. And while it may sound over the top to suggest that something as simple as a fall could constitute a crisis, the data and statistics back it up:

What’s even more alarming is the simple fact that many family doctors or general practitioners don’t know how to help their patients who fall. Generally, they give simple advice like, "try to be more careful,” or “make sure you keep your living area tidy and remove tripping hazards."

And while that advice is good advice, which should be followed, there’s so much more that can be done.

…That Comes Close To Home

As far-reaching as the impact of falls can be on our healthcare system, the impacts can also be deeply personal. For Janice, it meant a difficult conversation with her father and tough decisions about what he could do to keep from falling again. For her father, it meant watching his independence start to slip away.

 
"Falls, with or without injury, also carry a heavy quality of life impact. A growing number of older adults fear falling and, as a result, limit their activities and social engagements. This can result in further physical decline, depression, social isolation and feelings of helplessness." – National Council on Aging
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
 

Janice’s father was a proud man and didn’t want his daughter to worry about him. That’s why he didn’t tell her that he stopped going on his daily walks through the local park, opting instead to stay home for fear of falling in public. And while at home, he started spending more and more of his time indoors, away from his gardening.

 
"Each year, over 800,000 people are hospitalized due to falls." – National Council on Aging
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
 

For many who fall, the emotional impact of the fall can be the worst part: bruises and soreness will eventually go away, and even broken bones can heal. But the fear and anxiety of falling again can be permanent, and it can keep people from living an active and independent life.

How a Pair of Shoes Can Help

Footwear has long been considered a risk factor in falls. Ill-fitting shoes, shoes that don’t provide an appropriate level of support or slip-resistance and shoes that are improperly worn can increase the likelihood of a fall. In fact, a 2008 study found that "footwear has been identified as a risk factor for both indoor and outdoor falls."

The idea of getting a better pair of shoes for her father intrigued Janice, so she started asking friends for suggestions.
